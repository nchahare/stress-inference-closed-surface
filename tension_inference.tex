\section{Elastostatics of a pressurised shape: problem setting and the
landscape of methods}
\label{sec:elastostatics}

Before the geometric and numerical machinery of the later sections, it is worth
placing the problem in the broader context of elastostatics: \emph{given a
hollow, pressurised shape, how does one compute the tension carried by its
wall, and why is the method used here (equilibrium-based inference on the
observed surface) the natural choice?}  This section answers that at the level
of the governing physics; the precise equations and their discretisation follow
in Sections~\ref{sec:geometry}--\ref{sec:gfdm}.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{What problem class this is}

A hollow pressurised shape is a \emph{thin-walled shell under internal
pressure}.  The quantity of interest — the ``tension'' — is the in-plane
\emph{stress resultant} $\Nb$: the force per unit length carried within the
wall, obtained by integrating the Cauchy stress $\bm\sigma$ through the wall
thickness $t$,
\begin{equation}
  \Nb = \int_{-t/2}^{+t/2}\bm\sigma\,ds \;\approx\; \bm\sigma\,t
  \quad(\text{thin wall}),
  \label{eq:el-resultant}
\end{equation}
so that $\Nb$ has units of force/length (N/m) and the principal stresses are
recovered by $\sigma_i=N_i/t$.  This is exactly the object defined in
\S\ref{sec:model}.

The first modelling fork is \emph{membrane} versus \emph{shell} behaviour:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item A \textbf{membrane} carries only in-plane resultants $(N_{11},N_{22},
        N_{12})$ — no bending moments.  Valid when the wall is thin and away
        from boundary layers, sharp features, or thickness discontinuities.
  \item A \textbf{shell} additionally carries bending moments and transverse
        shear.  These matter in thin boundary layers of width $\sim\!\sqrt{Rt}$
        near clamped edges, point loads, or rapid curvature changes.
\end{itemize}
For a smoothly curved, thin, pressurised closed surface the membrane idealisation
captures essentially all of the load, and is the regime adopted throughout these
notes (assumptions stated in \S\ref{sec:model}).

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The governing equation and its two projections}

Membrane equilibrium is a single vector PDE on the surface,
$\divs\Nb+\Delta p\,\nn=\vv0$ (derived in \S\ref{sec:balance}).  Its power is
that it separates by geometry into two physically distinct pieces:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item the \textbf{normal projection} gives the Young--Laplace law
        $N_{11}\kone+N_{22}\ktwo=\Delta p$ (\S\ref{sec:normal}); for a sphere
        this is the familiar $2N/R=\Delta p\Rightarrow N=\Delta p R/2$;
  \item the \textbf{tangential projection} gives in-plane equilibrium,
        $\divs\Nb|_{\rm tangent}=\vv0$ (\S\ref{sec:tangential}) — trivial for an
        isotropic field on a sphere, but the constraint that \emph{forces} the
        hoop/meridional anisotropy on a spheroid.
\end{itemize}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Static determinacy: why tension is geometric, not material}
\label{sec:el-determinacy}

The reason the tension can be computed \emph{without knowing the material} is
that the membrane problem is \emph{statically determinate}.  Counting per
surface point: the symmetric resultant tensor $\Nb$ has three independent
components, and equilibrium supplies three scalar equations (one normal, two
tangential).  Three equations, three unknowns — the resultant field is fixed by
geometry and pressure alone.  No stress--strain relation, no Young's modulus or
Poisson ratio, no reference (unstressed) configuration, and no strain measure
enter.  This is not an approximation but a structural property of membrane
equilibrium, and it is the entire basis of stress \emph{inference} (cMSM and the
present method); see also the thickness discussion in \S\ref{sec:thickness}.

The contrast makes the point sharp: a \emph{bending} shell carries six stress
quantities (three resultants $+$ three moments) but still only three equilibrium
equations, so it is statically \emph{indeterminate} and one is forced to bring in
kinematics and a constitutive law.  The moment bending matters, material matters.

Three caveats temper the determinacy — each is treated in detail later:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
  \item \textbf{No boundary $\Rightarrow$ self-equilibrating modes.}  A closed
        surface has no edge to anchor the solution, so the homogeneous problem
        $\divs\Nb=\vv0$ admits non-trivial states.  On a genus-0 surface there is
        no smooth divergence-free traceless tensor field, so on the sphere
        $\sigma_1=\sigma_2$ \emph{exactly} and any anisotropy is discretisation
        error (the null modes of \S\ref{sec:null-modes}).
  \item \textbf{Determinacy is geometry-dependent.}  The membrane PDE is elliptic
        where the Gaussian curvature $K>0$ (convex, well posed) but hyperbolic
        where $K<0$ (saddle regions), where characteristics appear and the local
        problem can be ill posed.  Negative-curvature embryo folds are exactly
        where inference becomes delicate
        (\S\ref{sec:resolution-requirement}).
  \item \textbf{Thickness is only a divisor.}  Because $\Nb$ is fixed by
        equilibrium, $\sigma=\Nb/t$ — thickness rescales the stress but cannot
        change the resultant.  This is why a spatially varying $t(\vv x)$ needs no
        re-solve, and why the uniform-vs-varying-thickness science lives in the
        forward FEM rather than in the inference (\S\ref{sec:thickness}).
\end{enumerate}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{How people actually compute tension: four families}
\label{sec:el-methods}

\paragraph{(a) Analytic membrane theory (axisymmetric).}
For a body of revolution under axisymmetric loading the equations collapse to
closed form.  Two relations do everything: the Laplace law
$N_\phi/r_1+N_\theta/r_2=\Delta p$ and the axial cap balance
$N_\phi=\Delta p\,r_2/2$.  These yield the sphere, cylinder, spheroid and torus
exactly.  This is the ``Local'' method (M1) of the results matrix.  Classic
references: Timoshenko \& Woinowsky-Krieger, \emph{Theory of Plates and Shells};
Fl\"ugge, \emph{Stresses in Shells}; Calladine, \emph{Theory of Shell
Structures}.  Limited to revolutions with symmetric loading.

\paragraph{(b) Pressure-vessel formulae.}
Engineering specialisations of (a): hoop stress $\sigma=pr/t$ and longitudinal
$pr/2t$ for a cylinder, $pr/2t$ for a sphere.  Same physics, lookup-table form.

\paragraph{(c) Forward elasticity FEM (the general-shape workhorse).}
Discretise the wall (continuum or shell elements), supply a constitutive law
\emph{and} a reference configuration, apply the pressure, solve the (generally
nonlinear) boundary-value problem for the displacement, then recover the stress.
For soft tissue this means finite-strain hyperelasticity (neo-Hookean, Ogden)
with large-deformation kinematics.  This is the M3 cross-check.  Its defining
trait is that it solves the \emph{forward} problem — reference shape $+$ material
$+$ load $\Rightarrow$ deformed shape $+$ stress — and therefore \emph{requires}
material parameters and the unstressed geometry, neither of which is measured for
an embryo.  Hence FEM is a validation, not the primary method.

\paragraph{(d) Inverse / inference methods (the niche of this work).}
One observes the \emph{current} (deformed) shape and the pressure but knows
neither the reference configuration nor the material.  Exploiting static
determinacy (\S\ref{sec:el-determinacy}), equilibrium is solved directly on the
observed surface.  Curved Monolayer Stress Microscopy (cMSM; Mar\'in-Llaurad\'o
\textit{et al.}, 2023) does this with a globally parametrised linear-triangle FEM
and is restricted to open domes; the present GFDM approach uses a local-frame
finite-difference discretisation and generalises to \emph{closed / arbitrary}
topology, which is the methodological contribution.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The constitutive ladder and where this method sits}
\label{sec:el-ladder}

The whole field organises cleanly by ``how much physics beyond equilibrium one
is forced to add'':

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Level} & \textbf{What is added} & \textbf{Determinate?} & \textbf{Needs material?} \\
\midrule
Membrane equilibrium       & nothing (geometry $+\,\Delta p$)        & yes$^\ast$ & no \\
$+$ linear elasticity      & small-strain Hooke, kinematics          & ---        & yes \\
$+$ bending (shell)        & moments $\mathbf M$                     & no         & yes \\
$+$ finite-strain hyperel. & neo-Hookean / Ogden                     & no         & yes \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\noindent{\small $^\ast$ Determinate pointwise; closed surfaces retain a
finite-dimensional self-equilibrating null space (\S\ref{sec:null-modes}).}

\medskip
The tension-inference pipeline deliberately stays on the \emph{top rung} (methods
M1/M2) and descends to the bottom rung only for the independent FEM cross-check
(M3).  The honest scientific consequence — because tension is statically
determinate — is that the interesting material/thickness coupling (a
dorsoventral-varying $t$) can \emph{only} be probed at the FEM level: at the
membrane level $t$ is a passive divisor (\S\ref{sec:thickness}).

This also explains why the present method exists at all.  The ``usual'' route for
a known, simple, axisymmetric shape is analytic membrane theory (M1); the
``usual'' route for an arbitrary shape with known material is forward FEM (M3).
The problem here is neither: an \emph{arbitrary closed shape with unknown material
observed in its loaded state}.  That box is filled only by equilibrium-based
inference on the observed surface (M2), and showing that all three methods agree
on the sphere and spheroid — where each is computable — is what licenses using
M2 alone on the embryo.

\section{Surface geometry and local frame}
\label{sec:geometry}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Parametrisation and covariant basis}

Let $\Gamma\subset\R^3$ be a smooth, orientable surface (the midsurface of the
membrane wall).  We parametrise $\Gamma$ by the mapping
\begin{equation}
  \vv{x} = \varphi(\bm{\xi}), \qquad \bm{\xi}=(\xi^1,\xi^2)\in\Gamma_0\subset\R^2.
  \label{eq:param}
\end{equation}
The \emph{covariant basis vectors} tangent to $\Gamma$ are
\begin{equation}
  \vv{e}_a = \frac{\partial\vv{x}}{\partial\xi^a}, \qquad a\in\{1,2\}.
  \label{eq:cov-basis}
\end{equation}
These span the tangent plane $T_{\vv x}\Gamma$ at every point.  The
\emph{first fundamental form} (metric tensor) is
\begin{equation}
  g_{ab} = \vv{e}_a\cdot\vv{e}_b.
  \label{eq:metric}
\end{equation}
The unit outward normal is
\begin{equation}
  \nn = \frac{\vv{e}_1\times\vv{e}_2}{|\vv{e}_1\times\vv{e}_2|}.
  \label{eq:normal}
\end{equation}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Second fundamental form and shape operator}

The \emph{second fundamental form} encodes how the normal $\nn$ tilts as one
moves along the surface:
\begin{equation}
  b_{ab} = \nn\cdot\frac{\partial^2\vv{x}}{\partial\xi^a\,\partial\xi^b}
          = -\frac{\partial\nn}{\partial\xi^a}\cdot\vv{e}_b.
  \label{eq:IIF}
\end{equation}
(Both expressions are equivalent by the Weingarten equation.)
The \emph{shape operator} (Weingarten map) is the mixed tensor
$W^a{}_b = g^{ac}b_{cb}$, whose eigenvalues are the \emph{principal curvatures}
$\kone\ge\ktwo$ and whose eigenvectors define the \emph{principal directions}.

The mean and Gaussian curvatures are
\begin{equation}
  \Hmean = \tfrac12(\kone+\ktwo), \qquad
  \Kgauss = \kone\ktwo.
  \label{eq:HK}
\end{equation}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Principal curvature frame}
\label{sec:frame}

At a non-umbilic point ($\kone\ne\ktwo$) there exists a unique pair of mutually
orthogonal unit tangent vectors $\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc},\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}$ aligned
with the principal directions.  Together with the outward normal, they form the
\emph{principal curvature frame}:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{%
  \bigl(\,\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc},\;\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc},\;\nn\,\bigr),
  \qquad
  \vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}\times\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}=\nn,
  }
  \label{eq:pc-frame}
\end{equation}
an orthonormal right-handed basis of $\R^3$.  At an umbilic point
($\kone=\ktwo$) any orthonormal pair in the tangent plane serves as the frame.

\section{Membrane stress model}
\label{sec:model}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Assumptions}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Thin wall.}  Wall thickness $t$ satisfies
        $t\ll\min(R_1,R_2)$ with $R_i=1/|\kappa_i|$, so bending moments are
        negligible and stress is uniform through the thickness.
  \item \textbf{Membrane state of stress.}  The wall stress is purely in-plane
        (tangential to $\Gamma$); there are no transverse shear or normal
        stresses.
  \item \textbf{Quasi-static equilibrium.}  Inertial forces are zero.
  \item \textbf{No tangential body force.}  The only applied load is a
        normal pressure jump $\Delta p = p_{\rm in}-p_{\rm out}>0$.
\end{enumerate}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Stress resultant tensor}

The Cauchy stress integrated through the thickness gives the \emph{membrane
stress-resultant tensor} (force per unit length):
\begin{equation}
  \Nb = \int_{-t/2}^{+t/2}\bm{\sigma}\,ds,
  \qquad \Nb\,\nn = \vv{0}.
  \label{eq:resultant}
\end{equation}
The constraint $\Nb\,\nn=\vv{0}$ states that $\Nb$ is tangential.  In the
principal curvature frame it takes the form
\begin{equation}
  \Nb
    = N_{11}\,\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}\tp\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}
    + N_{22}\,\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}\tp\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}
    + N_{12}\bigl(\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}\tp\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}
                 +\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}\tp\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}\bigr),
  \label{eq:N-decomp}
\end{equation}
with three independent scalar DOFs $(N_{11},N_{22},N_{12})$ per vertex.  The
corresponding \emph{principal membrane stresses} are
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_1 = \frac{N_{11}}{t}, \qquad \sigma_2 = \frac{N_{22}}{t}.
  \label{eq:sigma}
\end{equation}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The role of thickness: resultant versus stress}
\label{sec:thickness}

Equations~\eqref{eq:resultant}--\eqref{eq:sigma} split the problem into two
stages whose dependence on the thickness $t$ is very different, and it is worth
stating precisely where $t$ does and does not enter.

\paragraph{The solve never sees the thickness.}
The equilibrium equation~\eqref{eq:equil}, and hence the entire linear system we
assemble in \S\ref{sec:gfdm}, is written purely in terms of the \emph{resultant}
$\Nb$ (force per unit length) and contains no $t$.  Geometry — the curvatures
$\kappa_1,\kappa_2$ and the surface differential operators — together with the
load $\Delta p$ fix $\Nb$ completely.  Thickness enters only afterwards, through
the pointwise division $\sigma_i = N_{ii}/t$ in equation~\eqref{eq:sigma}.

\paragraph{Why this is exact, not an approximation: static determinacy.}
The membrane problem on a pressurised closed surface is \emph{statically
determinate}.  At each vertex there are three equilibrium equations — one normal
(\S\ref{sec:normal}) and two tangential (\S\ref{sec:tangential}) — for the three
stress DOFs $(N_{11},N_{22},N_{12})$.  No constitutive (stress--strain) relation
is required to close the system, so the resultant field $\Nb$ is independent of
both the material model and the thickness distribution.  The classical sphere
result $\sigma=\Delta p\,R/2t$ is the canonical instance: it follows from force
balance alone, with $t$ appearing only as a final divisor.  This is exactly the
constitutive-free property that the cMSM force-inference method relies upon.

\begin{quote}\small
\textbf{Remark (two distinct meanings of ``determinacy'').}  The
\emph{constitutive} determinacy used here — $\Nb$ is fixed without a material
law — should not be confused with the \emph{static indeterminacy / null modes}
of \S\ref{sec:null-modes}.  The former holds pointwise from the differential
equilibrium equations; the latter is a global property of a \emph{closed}
surface, where the discrete operator $L$ admits self-equilibrating null modes
and the numerical problem becomes rank-deficient.  Thickness plays no role in
either.
\end{quote}

\paragraph{Consequence for spatially varying thickness.}
Because $\Nb$ does not depend on $t$, a spatially varying field
$t=t(\vv{x})$ requires \emph{no} re-solve.  One stores the resultants
$N_1,N_2$ from a single solve and recovers the stresses by the pointwise
division
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_i(\vv{x}) = \frac{N_i}{t(\vv{x})}, \qquad i=1,2.
  \label{eq:sigma-varying}
\end{equation}
In practice this means: (i) the resultants $N_1,N_2$ are the durable output of
the inverse solve and are stored alongside $\sigma_1,\sigma_2$; (ii) a new
thickness map only changes the divisor in~\eqref{eq:sigma-varying}; and
(iii) the thin-wall assumption must be re-checked pointwise, flagging vertices
where $t(\vv{x})\gtrsim 0.1\,\min(R_1,R_2)$.

\paragraph{When thickness does more than divide.}
The pointwise scaling is special to the \emph{inverse} membrane problem on a
prescribed geometry.  Thickness couples non-trivially in other regimes:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Forward elastic shells.}  If one predicts the deformed shape
        from a reference configuration and a material law, the resultant becomes
        $\Nb = t\,\mathbb{C}{:}\,\bm{\varepsilon}$, so $t$ enters the
        constitutive relation; the membrane stiffness scales as $t$ while the
        bending rigidity scales as $t^3$, and thickness \emph{gradients} add
        terms to the governing operator.  The dorsoventral-varying-thickness
        study (our M3 neo-Hookean FEM cross-check) lives in this regime — there
        $t$ genuinely changes the equilibrium shape and curvatures, not just the
        stress amplitude.
  \item \textbf{Fluid membranes (soap films, lipid bilayers).}  The tension is
        set thermodynamically (surface free energy), is uniform and isotropic
        because the fluid flows in-plane to equalise it, and is independent of
        the molecular film thickness.  Spatial tension variation, when present,
        is driven by a surfactant-concentration / Marangoni transport equation
        rather than by thickness; the nanometric film thickness affects optics
        and drainage, not the mechanical force balance.
\end{itemize}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Why the surface must be solid-like: the fluid versus elastic limit}
\label{sec:fluid-vs-elastic}

It is worth making explicit \emph{why} the stress tensor $\Nb$ of
\S\ref{sec:model} has three independent components in the first place, rather
than a single scalar tension.  The answer separates a soap bubble from an
epithelial shell, and it is the reason the inverse problem solved in these notes
is non-trivial.

\paragraph{The fluid limit: isotropic tension.}
A clean fluid interface cannot store in-plane shear, so its surface stress is
\emph{isotropic},
\begin{equation}
  \Nb = \gamma\,\mathbf P, \qquad \mathbf P = \mathbf I - \nn\otimes\nn,
  \label{eq:fluid-stress}
\end{equation}
with a single scalar tension $\gamma$ and $\mathbf P$ the tangential projector.
Substituting~\eqref{eq:fluid-stress} into the equilibrium
law~\eqref{eq:equil} and using
$\divs(\gamma\mathbf P)=\nabla_{\!s}\gamma+\gamma\,\divs\mathbf P$ with the
identity $\divs\mathbf P=-(\kone+\ktwo)\,\nn$ gives
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_{\!s}\gamma \;-\; \gamma(\kone+\ktwo)\,\nn \;+\; \Delta p\,\nn = \vv0,
  \label{eq:fluid-balance}
\end{equation}
whose normal and tangential projections are
\begin{equation}
  \underbrace{\gamma(\kone+\ktwo)=\Delta p}_{\text{normal: local Young--Laplace}},
  \qquad\qquad
  \underbrace{\nabla_{\!s}\gamma=\vv0}_{\text{tangential}}.
  \label{eq:fluid-projections}
\end{equation}
Since the enclosed pressure is uniform, the normal equation forces
$\kone+\ktwo=\Delta p/\gamma(\vv x)$: a low-tension patch must carry high mean
curvature (the interface bulges where $\gamma$ is small).  The tangential
equation is the decisive one --- a static fluid interface \emph{requires uniform
tension}.

\paragraph{A bubble homogenises its own tension.}
A tension gradient $\nabla_{\!s}\gamma$ is a tangential (Marangoni) force per
unit area, and a fluid has no in-plane shear stress to oppose it.  It is
therefore not an equilibrium at all: the gradient drives Marangoni flow that
transports surfactant (or heat) until $\gamma$ equalises, or until a steady
state in which a \emph{bulk} viscous traction balances it.  A heterogeneous
surface tension on a true bubble is thus an intrinsically dynamic, non-static
state.  For it to persist statically, the tangential force must be balanced by
something able to carry tangential stress: surface (Gibbs) elasticity, surface
viscosity in steady flow, a bulk shear traction, or a genuinely solid-like
elastic skin.

\paragraph{The elastic limit: heterogeneity forces anisotropy.}
An epithelial cortex (or the aneurysm wall of the bulge-inflation experiments)
\emph{can} carry shear, so $\Nb$ is a full symmetric tensor.  Splitting it into
an isotropic tension plus a deviatoric part, $\Nb=\gamma(\vv x)\mathbf P+\Nb_{\rm
dev}$, the tangential balance becomes
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_{\!s}\gamma + \bigl(\divs\Nb_{\rm dev}\bigr)\big|_{\rm tangent} = \vv0 .
  \label{eq:het-forces-aniso}
\end{equation}
Hence a spatially varying scalar tension \emph{cannot remain isotropic} at
equilibrium: its gradient must be balanced by the divergence of a deviatoric
(shear) stress.  Heterogeneity of the mean tension and anisotropy of the stress
tensor are not independent --- equilibrium couples them.  This is exactly why,
on a general surface, the shear DOF $r\ne0$ and the principal-stress axes tilt
off the curvature axes (Sections~\ref{sec:pc-frame-solve}
and~\ref{sec:stress-dirs}): a heterogeneous tension landscape \emph{is} an
anisotropic stress field.

\paragraph{Why this makes the inverse problem well posed.}
The same contrast re-derives the static determinacy of
\S\ref{sec:el-determinacy} from the counting of unknowns:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Fluid surface} --- one unknown $\gamma$ per point.  The normal
        equation alone fixes it, and the tangential
        equation~\eqref{eq:fluid-projections} then over-constrains it to be
        uniform.  There is nothing to infer.
  \item \textbf{Elastic membrane} --- three unknowns $(p,q,r)$ per point against
        three equilibrium equations.  Statically determinate, and able to carry a
        heterogeneous, anisotropic stress field.
\end{itemize}
The membrane-stress inference of these notes is meaningful precisely because the
biological surface is solid-like rather than fluid: only then does a structured,
time-stable tension landscape exist to be recovered.

\section{Balance of linear momentum}
\label{sec:balance}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Vector equilibrium equation}

The membrane equilibrium follows from the balance of forces on an infinitesimal
surface patch.  In Cauchy form on the surface:
\begin{equation}
  \divs\Nb + \Delta p\,\nn = \vv{0}.
  \label{eq:equil}
\end{equation}
Here $\divs$ is the \emph{surface divergence}: for a surface tensor field
$\Nb$, $(\divs\Nb)^i = \nabla_\alpha N^{\alpha i}$ where $\nabla_\alpha$ is
the covariant surface derivative along $\vv{e}_\alpha$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:equil} is a vector equation in $\R^3$.  Projecting onto
$\nn$ and onto the two tangent directions separates it into one \emph{normal}
and two \emph{tangential} scalar equations.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Normal component --- the Laplace (Young--Laplace) law}
\label{sec:normal}

Taking the dot product of~\eqref{eq:equil} with $\nn$ and using the identity
$(\divs\Nb)\cdot\nn = -\Nb:B$ (where $B=[b_{ab}]$ is the second fundamental
form regarded as a bilinear form), we obtain
\begin{equation}
  \Nb : B = \Delta p,
  \label{eq:laplace-tensor}
\end{equation}
i.e.\ $N^{ab}b_{ab}=\Delta p$.  In the principal curvature frame
($b_{ab}=\mathrm{diag}(\kone,\ktwo)$ and $N_{12}=0$ on the principal axes) this
reduces to the classical \textbf{Young--Laplace law}:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{N_{11}\,\kone + N_{22}\,\ktwo = \Delta p,}
  \label{eq:laplace}
\end{equation}
or equivalently, in terms of principal stresses and thickness,
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_1\,\kone + \sigma_2\,\ktwo = \frac{\Delta p}{t}.
  \label{eq:laplace-sigma}
\end{equation}
This is the \emph{normal projection} of the equilibrium: one scalar equation
per surface point relating the two unknown stress components to the pressure
load and the local curvatures.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Tangential components --- in-plane equilibrium}
\label{sec:tangential}

Projecting~\eqref{eq:equil} onto the two tangent directions $\vv{e}_\alpha^{\rm
pc}$ ($\alpha=1,2$) and using $\Delta p\,\nn\cdot\vv{e}_\alpha^{\rm pc}=0$ gives
\begin{equation}
  \bigl(\divs\Nb\bigr)\cdot\vv{e}_\alpha^{\rm pc} = 0, \qquad \alpha=1,2.
  \label{eq:tang-equil}
\end{equation}
In local coordinates these expand to:
\begin{align}
  \frac{\partial N_{11}}{\partial s_1}
  + \frac{\partial N_{12}}{\partial s_2}
  + (\kone-\ktwo)\,N_{12}\,c_{\alpha\beta}
  &= 0, \label{eq:tang1} \\[4pt]
  \frac{\partial N_{12}}{\partial s_1}
  + \frac{\partial N_{22}}{\partial s_2}
  + (\kone-\ktwo)\,N_{12}\,c_{\alpha\beta}
  &= 0, \label{eq:tang2}
\end{align}
where $\partial/\partial s_\alpha$ denotes the arc-length derivative along
$\vv{e}_\alpha^{\rm pc}$ and the curvature-coupling terms arise from the
Christoffel symbols of the surface connection.

\paragraph{On closed surfaces.}  Equations~\eqref{eq:laplace} and
\eqref{eq:tang-equil} together form a $3n$-dimensional system for $(N_{11},
N_{22},N_{12})$ at every vertex.  The system is \emph{statically indeterminate}
on a closed surface (genus 0): there exists a finite-dimensional space of
self-equilibrating (null) stress states.  These are suppressed by Tikhonov
regularisation (Section~\ref{sec:gfdm}).

%% =========================================================================
\section{Ambient-component form and the GFDM discretisation}
\label{sec:gfdm}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Working in world Cartesian components}

A key simplification arises when $\Nb$ is expressed in its \emph{ambient
Cartesian components} $N^{ij}$ ($i,j\in\{1,2,3\}$) rather than covariant
surface components.  The surface divergence then reads
\begin{equation}
  \bigl(\divs\Nb\bigr)^i
    = \sum_j\Bigl[
        (v_1)_j\,\partial_\xi N^{ij} + (v_2)_j\,\partial_\eta N^{ij}
      \Bigr],
  \label{eq:divs-ambient}
\end{equation}
where $(v_1,v_2,\nn)$ is any local orthonormal tangent frame and
$\partial_\xi,\partial_\eta$ are the surface derivatives along $v_1,v_2$.  The
Christoffel symbols (surface connection terms) are \emph{absorbed into the
ambient representation}: they do not appear explicitly, which makes the
formulation valid on arbitrary surfaces without coordinate charts.

\paragraph{Constraint $\Nb\,\nn=\vv 0$.}  In the local frame $(v_1,v_2)$ the
constraint is automatically satisfied by the parameterisation
\begin{equation}
  \Nb = p\,(v_1\tp v_1) + q\,(v_2\tp v_2) + r\,(v_1\tp v_2 + v_2\tp v_1),
  \label{eq:dofs}
\end{equation}
giving 3 scalar DOFs $(p,q,r)$ per vertex.  Principal stresses are eigenvalues
of the $2\times 2$ matrix $\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}p&r\\r&q\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$
divided by $t$.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Generalised finite-difference (GFDM) operators}

At each vertex $i$ with local frame $(v_1^{(i)},v_2^{(i)},\nn^{(i)})$ we
gather the $k$-ring neighbourhood $\mathcal{N}(i)$ (ring depth $d=3$ by
default, giving $\sim\!30$ neighbours).  Neighbour positions are projected into
the tangent plane as $(\xi_j,\eta_j)$ coordinates.  We then solve a
weighted least-squares quadratic Taylor expansion
\begin{equation}
  f_j - f_i \;\approx\;
    a_i\xi_j + b_i\eta_j
    + \tfrac12 c_i\xi_j^2 + d_i\xi_j\eta_j + \tfrac12 e_i\eta_j^2,
  \label{eq:taylor}
\end{equation}
for the 5 coefficients $(a_i,\dots,e_i)$.  The first-order coefficients give
\emph{stencil weights} that assemble into sparse $n\times n$ matrices:
\begin{equation}
  (G_\xi f)_i \approx \frac{\partial f}{\partial\xi}\Big|_i,
  \qquad
  (G_\eta f)_i \approx \frac{\partial f}{\partial\eta}\Big|_i.
  \label{eq:gfdm-ops}
\end{equation}
Similarly, the second-order coefficients yield $H_{\xi\xi},H_{\xi\eta},H_{\eta\eta}$.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Solve in the principal curvature frame}
\label{sec:pc-frame-solve}

An alternative, physically transparent parameterisation replaces the arbitrary
fit frame $(v_1,v_2)$ with the \emph{principal curvature frame}
$(\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc},\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc})$ from Section~\ref{sec:pc-frame}.
The DOF expansion becomes
\begin{equation}
  \Nb = p\,(\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}\tp\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc})
      + q\,(\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}\tp\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc})
      + r\,(\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}\tp\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}
           +\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}\tp\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}),
  \label{eq:dofs-pc}
\end{equation}
so that $p\approx\sigma_1 t$ and $q\approx\sigma_2 t$ \emph{directly} on
axisymmetric surfaces (where symmetry forces $r=0$).  On a general surface
$r\ne 0$ and the principal stresses still require diagonalising
$\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}p&r\\r&q\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.  The final
$\sigma_1,\sigma_2$ values are \textbf{identical} to those obtained with any
other tangent basis, because eigenvalues of $\Nb$ are frame-independent.

The shear component $r$ quantifies the \emph{alignment} between the stress and
curvature axes: $|r|/(|p|+|q|)\approx 1$--$2\%$ on the sphere and spheroid
(where axisymmetry forces near-coincidence) and becomes non-trivial on
general closed surfaces.

Note: BFS sign propagation of $\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}$ (Section~\ref{sec:sign})
has unavoidable singularities at umbilic points, which locally perturb the
GFDM stencils built from $(\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc},\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc})$.  For
totally umbilic surfaces (e.g.\ the sphere) this is severe; for surfaces with
only isolated umbilic points (spheroid, capsule, real meshes) the effect is
localised to $\le 3$ vertices.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Assembly: a square system}
\label{sec:assembly}

Substituting~\eqref{eq:dofs} into~\eqref{eq:divs-ambient} and
\eqref{eq:equil} yields, at each vertex $i$, three scalar equations —
one for each ambient Cartesian component of the vector
equilibrium~\eqref{eq:equil}.  With $n$ vertices and 3 DOFs per vertex,
the global system is \emph{square}:
\begin{equation}
  L\,\vv{s} = \vv{b}, \qquad
  L\in\mathbb{R}^{3n\times 3n}, \quad
  \vv{b}^{(i)} = -\Delta p\,\nn^{(i)}, \quad
  \vv{s}^{(i)} = (p_i,q_i,r_i).
  \label{eq:system}
\end{equation}
At first glance this looks fully determined: 3 equations per vertex
(1 normal + 2 tangential) for 3 unknowns per vertex $(p,q,r)$.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Static indeterminacy and null modes on closed surfaces}
\label{sec:null-modes}

Despite the $3n\times 3n$ size, the system matrix $L$ is
\textbf{rank-deficient} on a closed surface.  This is because there
exists a finite-dimensional space of \emph{self-equilibrating stress
states} — non-zero solutions $\vv{s}_{\rm null}$ that satisfy
$L\,\vv{s}_{\rm null}=\vv{0}$ even in the absence of any external load.

Physically, these null modes are internal stress distributions that
exactly balance themselves around the closed surface without requiring any
pressure to sustain them.  Because the surface has no boundary edges to
anchor a unique solution, any valid stress field $\vv{s}^*$ can be augmented
by any amount of $\vv{s}_{\rm null}$ without violating equilibrium:
\[
  L(\vv{s}^*+\alpha\,\vv{s}_{\rm null}) = L\vv{s}^* + \alpha\,L\vv{s}_{\rm null} = \vv{b}.
\]
This gives infinitely many solutions.

\paragraph{The null-mode pattern is mesh-dependent.}
The \emph{existence} of null modes is a topological fact: any closed
genus-0 surface has self-equilibrating stress states regardless of how
it is triangulated.  However, the \emph{spatial pattern} of those modes
is set by the mesh structure.  On an IcoSphere the null modes align with
the icosahedral triangulation — its 5-fold and 6-fold symmetry axes —
producing the characteristic streaks visible in the raw GFDM output.
On a UV sphere they would align with meridians and parallels; on a real
biological mesh they follow the local edge directions.  In short, the
streaks are a fingerprint of the triangulation, not of the biology.

A practical consequence: finer meshes push the null-mode pattern to
higher spatial frequency, which is why Laplacian post-smoothing is more
effective at subdiv-5 than subdiv-4, and why the IcoSphere is preferred
over a UV sphere (the IcoSphere's uniform triangulation avoids the
pole-concentrated artefacts of the UV grid).

\paragraph{Effect on the principal stresses: a one-sided bias.}
Because the null modes are self-equilibrating, their net contribution to
the mean stress $(\sigma_1+\sigma_2)/2$ is zero: any spurious tension at
one vertex is cancelled by compression at a neighbouring vertex.  The
mean stress is therefore an unbiased estimator of the true isotropic
load even before smoothing.

The maximum principal stress $\sigma_1$, however, is systematically
biased \emph{upward}.  Each null mode adds a localised deviatoric
perturbation $\delta_i$ at vertex $i$, turning the principal stresses
into $\sigma_{\rm ref}\pm|\delta_i|$.  Taking the larger eigenvalue
always picks up the positive side:
\begin{equation}
  \langle\sigma_1\rangle
    = \sigma_{\rm ref} + \langle|\delta|\rangle > \sigma_{\rm ref},
  \qquad
  \langle\sigma_2\rangle
    = \sigma_{\rm ref} - \langle|\delta|\rangle < \sigma_{\rm ref},
  \qquad
  \langle\tfrac{\sigma_1+\sigma_2}{2}\rangle = \sigma_{\rm ref}.
  \label{eq:rician-bias}
\end{equation}
This is the same mechanism as \emph{Rician bias} in MRI: taking a
magnitude (always non-negative) from a noisy signal gives a mean that
is biased upward even when the underlying signal is symmetric.  On the
IcoSphere subdiv-4 the bias amounts to ${\sim}6\%$ of the true
$\sigma_1$ in the raw solve; Laplacian post-smoothing reduces
$\langle|\delta|\rangle$ by ${\sim}8\times$, bringing the bias down to
${\sim}1\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}).

Because the null-mode amplitude scales with $\Delta p$ (the modes are
driven by the right-hand side $\vv{b}=-\Delta p\,\nn$), the relative
bias $\langle|\delta|\rangle/\sigma_{\rm ref}$ is a \emph{constant} of the
mesh and solve parameters — independent of $\Delta p$ or $t$.  This is
why the linearity test (§\ref{sec:validation-checks}) shows a fixed ratio
$\sigma_1/(\Delta p/t)=0.5316$ rather than the analytic $0.500$: the
6\% offset is a systematic null-mode bias, not a linearity error.

\paragraph{Contrast with open surfaces.}  On an open dome (as in the
reference cMSM method), the boundary conditions pin the stress at the
boundary, removing the null space entirely.  Closed surfaces have no
such anchor, making the problem intrinsically more difficult.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Tikhonov regularisation}
\label{sec:tikhonov}

Because $L$ is rank-deficient, a direct solve fails (or returns a
numerically arbitrary result dominated by the null modes).  We instead
minimise the Tikhonov-regularised objective
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\vv{s}}\;\bigl\|L\vv{s}-\vv{b}\bigr\|^2
  + \lambda^2\,\bigl\|R\vv{s}\bigr\|^2,
  \label{eq:tikhonovobjective}
\end{equation}
whose normal equations are
\begin{equation}
  (L^\top L + \lambda^2 R^\top R)\,\vv{s} = L^\top\vv{b},
  \label{eq:tikhonov}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $R$ is a \emph{roughness} (smoothness) regulariser — in practice
        the discrete surface gradient of each ambient stress component, so
        that $\|R\vv{s}\|^2=\sum_{ab}\|\nabla_s N_{ab}\|^2$.
  \item $\lambda\approx0.01$--$0.05$ is a small penalty weight.
\end{itemize}

The regulariser acts as a \emph{tie-breaker}: among the infinitely many
solutions that satisfy $L\vv{s}=\vv{b}$, it selects the one with minimum
spatial variation.  The oscillatory null modes have high roughness, so
they are suppressed at the cost of a small bias in the smooth physical
modes.  The residual low-frequency artefacts are removed by a final
Laplacian post-smoothing pass
($\sigma\leftarrow(1-\alpha)\sigma+\alpha\bar\sigma_{1\text{-ring}}$,
$\alpha=0.5$, ${\sim}12$ iterations).

\paragraph{Solving the normal equations.}
For small meshes the symmetric system~\eqref{eq:tikhonov} is factored
directly with a sparse LU.  This is exact but scales poorly: the depth-3
GFDM stencils carry ${\sim}30$ non-zeros per row, so $L^\top L$ has
${\sim}900$ non-zeros per row and the factorisation suffers heavy fill-in
— empirically the solve time grows by ${\sim}\times35$ for each
${\times}4$ in the number of DOFs (\S\ref{sec:resolution-requirement},
Table~\ref{tab:timing}).  Above a DOF threshold we therefore switch to an
iterative least-squares solver (\texttt{lsqr}) applied directly to the
augmented system $[\,L\,;\,\lambda R\,]\vv{s}=[\,\vv{b}\,;\,\vv{0}\,]$,
which avoids forming $L^\top L$ and its fill-in and scales far more
gently.  Both solvers minimise the same objective~\eqref{eq:tikhonovobjective}
and agree to solver tolerance.  The crossover threshold should be set
around $20$--$30\,000$ DOFs (${\sim}7$--$10\,000$ vertices): beyond that
the iterative path is already faster than the direct one, so a higher
threshold needlessly leaves mid-size meshes on the slow direct solve.

\paragraph{An independent discretisation.}  The GFDM scheme above is only
\emph{one} way to discretise the balance law~\eqref{eq:equil}.  A stress-based
\emph{finite-element} discretisation of the same equation --- the natural twin of
this least-squares scheme, and a faithful version of the reference cMSM method on
a \emph{closed} surface --- is developed as a cross-check in
Section~\ref{sec:fem}.

\section{Extracting the principal curvature frame}
\label{sec:pc-frame}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Shape operator from the Weingarten map}

For each vertex $i$ with local frame $(v_1,v_2,\nn)$, the GFDM operators give
the surface gradient of the normal field $\nn$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\nn}{\partial\xi}\Big|_i = G_\xi\,\nn, \qquad
  \frac{\partial\nn}{\partial\eta}\Big|_i = G_\eta\,\nn.
  \label{eq:grad-n}
\end{equation}
The components of the second fundamental form in the local frame are then
\begin{align}
  B_{11} &= \frac{\partial\nn}{\partial\xi}\cdot v_1, &
  B_{22} &= \frac{\partial\nn}{\partial\eta}\cdot v_2, &
  B_{12} &= \tfrac12\!\left(\frac{\partial\nn}{\partial\xi}\cdot v_2
                           + \frac{\partial\nn}{\partial\eta}\cdot v_1\right).
  \label{eq:B-components}
\end{align}
(Sign: $+\mathrm{grad}_s\nn$, so $\mathrm{tr}\,B=2H>0$ for an outward normal
under positive pressure.)

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Analytic 2$\times$2 diagonalisation}

The shape operator is represented by the symmetric $2\times 2$ matrix
$\mathbf{B}=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}B_{11}&B_{12}\\B_{12}&B_{22}\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$
in the $(v_1,v_2)$ basis.  Its eigenvalues are the principal curvatures:
\begin{equation}
  H_{\rm loc} = \tfrac12(B_{11}+B_{22}), \quad
  d = \sqrt{\tfrac14(B_{11}-B_{22})^2 + B_{12}^2},
  \label{eq:eig-Hloc}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \kone = H_{\rm loc}+d \;\ge\; \ktwo = H_{\rm loc}-d.
  \label{eq:eig-kappas}
\end{equation}
The eigenvector for $\kone$ makes angle $\theta$ with $v_1$ in the tangent
plane, where
\begin{equation}
  \theta = \tfrac12\arctan\!\frac{2B_{12}}{B_{11}-B_{22}}.
  \label{eq:theta}
\end{equation}
The principal curvature directions in world $\R^3$ are then
\begin{equation}
  \vv{e}_1^{\rm pc} = \cos\theta\;v_1 + \sin\theta\;v_2, \qquad
  \vv{e}_2^{\rm pc} = \nn\times\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}.
  \label{eq:pc-dirs}
\end{equation}
The resulting frame $(\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc},\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc},\nn)$ is orthonormal
and right-handed by construction.  At umbilic points ($d=0$) the angle
$\theta=0$ by convention; any orthonormal pair in the tangent plane is equally
valid.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Sign ambiguity and global consistency}
\label{sec:sign}

\paragraph{The line-field problem.}
Each principal direction is defined only up to sign: $\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}$ and
$-\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}$ are physically equivalent.  The $\arctan2$ formula in
equation~\eqref{eq:theta} picks a sign at each vertex \emph{independently}.
Near a \emph{umbilic point} ($\kone\approx\ktwo$, $d\to0$), the discriminant
vanishes and $\arctan2$ is sensitive to floating-point noise: the sign chosen
at vertex $i$ can oppose that of its neighbour $j$, even though the true
direction varies smoothly.  Numerical experiments on the prolate spheroid
(IcoSphere, $\sim$2600 vertices) show that increasing the neighbourhood depth
$d_{\rm ring}$ from 2 to 5 does not reduce the number of sign-flipped vertices
(34--40 at all depths), confirming that the issue is geometric, not a
fit-accuracy problem.

\paragraph{BFS sign propagation.}
A single breadth-first traversal of the mesh graph (from an arbitrary seed
vertex) enforces a globally consistent orientation.  Starting from vertex 0
with its computed $\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}$, every unvisited neighbour $j$ of a
visited vertex $i$ is checked:
\begin{equation}
  \text{if } \vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}(i)\cdot\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}(j) < 0,\quad
  \text{then } \vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}(j)\leftarrow -\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}(j),\;
               \vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}(j)\leftarrow -\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}(j).
  \label{eq:bfs}
\end{equation}
Flipping both $\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}$ and $\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}$ simultaneously
preserves the right-handedness of the frame:
$(-\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc})\times(-\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc})=\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}\times\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc}=\nn$.
On the prolate spheroid, BFS reduces the number of sign-flipped 1-ring pairs
from 34 to 2.

\paragraph{Unavoidable topological singularities.}
The 2 residual flipped vertices correspond to the two \emph{umbilic points} at
the tips of the spheroid, where $\kone=\ktwo$ exactly.  These are genuine
topological singularities of the principal curvature line field: the
eigenvectors of $\mathbf{B}$ wind around such a point and no globally
consistent orientation exists in its neighbourhood.  This is not a numerical
artefact.

The Poincaré--Hopf theorem, applied to the principal curvature line field,
requires that the sum of singularity indices equals the Euler characteristic
$\chi(\Gamma)$ of the surface.  For a closed genus-0 surface
$\chi=2$; each regular umbilic point contributes index $+1$ to this sum.  The
prolate spheroid has exactly two umbilic points (both poles), each of index
$+1$, saturating the bound.  On an arbitrary smooth closed surface of genus 0
the Carath\'eodory conjecture asserts that there are always at least two
umbilic points; hence \textbf{at least 2 sign-propagation singularities are
unavoidable on any closed genus-0 mesh}, regardless of resolution or
neighbourhood size.

\section{Principal stress directions}
\label{sec:stress-dirs}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Curvature directions vs.\ stress directions}

The \emph{principal curvature directions} $(\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc},\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc})$
are a geometric property of the surface shape — eigenvectors of the shape
operator $\mathbf{B}$.  The \emph{principal stress directions} are the
eigenvectors of the stress tensor $\Nb$ at each vertex.  These two sets of
directions are \textbf{generally distinct}.

They coincide when the geometry and loading share the same symmetry.  For a
\emph{surface of revolution} under \emph{axisymmetric loading} (e.g.\ uniform
pressure on a sphere or spheroid), symmetry forces the principal stress
directions to align with the meridional and hoop directions — which are
simultaneously the eigenvectors of $\mathbf{B}$.  In this case the shear DOF
$r=0$ exactly (by symmetry), and $p=\sigma_1 t$, $q=\sigma_2 t$ directly.

On a \emph{general closed surface} no such symmetry exists.  The tangential
equilibrium equations $(\divs\Nb)\cdot\vv{e}_\alpha^{\rm pc}=0$ require
spatial gradients of the stress components to balance across the mesh; this
coupling forces $r\ne 0$ even in the principal curvature frame, meaning the
principal stress axes tilt away from the curvature axes.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Extracting principal stress directions from the solve}
\label{sec:d1d2}

After solving for $(p,q,r)$ in the principal curvature frame
(equation~\eqref{eq:dofs-pc}), the eigenvector of
$\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}p&r\\r&q\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$ for the larger
eigenvalue $N_1$ makes angle $\theta_s$ with $\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}$:
\begin{equation}
  \theta_s = \tfrac12\arctan\!\frac{2r}{p-q}.
  \label{eq:theta-s}
\end{equation}
The principal stress directions in world $\R^3$ are then
\begin{equation}
  \vv{d}_1 = \cos\theta_s\;\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc} + \sin\theta_s\;\vv{e}_2^{\rm pc},
  \qquad
  \vv{d}_2 = \nn\times\vv{d}_1.
  \label{eq:d1d2}
\end{equation}
These are unit vectors orthogonal to $\nn$ and to each other.  On axisymmetric
surfaces $r\approx 0$ so $\theta_s\approx 0$ and $\vv{d}_1\approx\vv{e}_1^{\rm pc}$;
on general surfaces $|\theta_s|>0$ quantifies the tilt.

The \emph{alignment diagnostic}
\begin{equation}
  \delta = \frac{|r|}{|p|+|q|}
  \label{eq:alignment}
\end{equation}
is $\approx 1$--$2\%$ on the sphere and spheroid (nearly aligned) and
will be non-trivially large on non-symmetric surfaces such as the
chick neural tube.

\section{Validation cases}
\label{sec:validation}

\paragraph{Sphere, $R=1$ (totally umbilic — worst case).}
All principal curvatures equal $1/R=1$; $H=1$, $K=1$ everywhere.  Because
$d\equiv0$ at every vertex, every point is umbilic and principal directions are
completely undefined: $\mathbf{B}=(1/R)\mathbf{I}_2$ so the $\arctan2$ formula
receives $(0,\,0)$ and the sign at each vertex is set by floating-point noise in
the GFDM estimate of $B_{12}$.  Frame orthonormality is still preserved to
machine precision ($|\vv{e}_1\cdot\vv{e}_2|\le10^{-16}$), but the
\emph{directions} are numerically arbitrary.

After BFS propagation the result is a smooth tangent vector field on $S^2$.
By the \textbf{hairy ball theorem} (a corollary of Poincaré--Hopf for $S^2$)
no continuous non-vanishing tangent vector field can exist on the sphere; the
BFS field must therefore develop singularities.  On an IcoSphere subdiv-4
($n=2562$ vertices), 314 vertices (12\% of the mesh) have more than 40\% of
their 1-ring neighbours pointing in the opposite direction after BFS —
forming broad singular \emph{patches} rather than isolated points, because the
entire surface is critical.  The curvature scalars ($\kone,\ktwo,H,K$) are
unaffected; only the directional output (\textbf{e}$_1$, \textbf{e}$_2$) is
meaningless on a totally umbilic surface.

\paragraph{Prolate spheroid, $a=2$, $b=1$ (long axis $x$).}
Away from the poles the surface is non-umbilic ($d>0$) and the principal
directions are well-defined.  At the equator ($|x|<0.1a$, $\sim\!1036$
vertices on IcoSphere subdiv-4):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrrr}
\toprule
Quantity & Computed (mean) & Std & Analytic \\
\midrule
$\kone$ (hoop)       & 1.004 & 0.028 & $1/b=1.000$ \\
$\ktwo$ (meridional) & 0.248 & 0.007 & $b/a^2=0.250$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Frame orthonormality: $|\vv{e}_1\cdot\vv{e}_2|\le2\times10^{-16}$ (machine
precision).  Membrane stresses: $\sigma_{\rm hoop}=\Delta p r_2(1-r_2/2r_1)/t$,
$\sigma_{\rm merid}=\Delta p r_2/2t$.

\paragraph{Capsule, $R=1$, $H=2$ (cylinder + hemispherical end-caps).}

A capsule is a cylinder of radius $R$ and half-height $H$ capped with two
hemispheres.  It has three geometrically distinct regions:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Cylindrical body} ($|z|<H$): $\kone=1/R$ (hoop), $\ktwo=0$ (axial).
        Non-umbilic; $d=1/(2R)$; principal directions well-defined.
  \item \textbf{Spherical caps} ($|z|>H+R\,\varepsilon$): $\kone=\ktwo=1/R$.
        Totally umbilic; $d=0$; same sign-propagation pathology as the sphere.
  \item \textbf{Junction} ($|z|\approx H$): $d$ ramps smoothly between the two values.
\end{itemize}
The parametric capsule mesh (\texttt{make\_capsule} in \texttt{show\_capsule.py},
$n_\theta=40$, $n_\varphi=14$, matched ring spacing) gives $n=2522$ vertices.

\begin{center}

\subsection{Pressure and thickness linearity}

For a statically-determinate membrane the stress scales exactly as
$\sigma\propto\Delta p/t$.  Concretely:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_1(\Delta p_2,\,t_2)
  = \frac{\Delta p_2/t_2}{\Delta p_1/t_1}\;\sigma_1(\Delta p_1,\,t_1).
\end{equation}

The test was run on the sphere ($R=1$) and prolate spheroid ($a=2$, $b=1$) at
six $(\Delta p,\,t)$ combinations spanning a factor of eight in $\Delta p/t$
(Figure~\ref{fig:linearity}).  Two routes reach the same $\Delta p/t$: varying
$\Delta p$ at fixed $t=0.05$ (circles), and varying $t$ at fixed $\Delta p=20$\,Pa
(squares).

\begin{figure}[ht]\centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{out/linearity_test.png}
  \caption{Linearity test: mean $\sigma_1$ vs $\Delta p/t$ for the sphere (left)
  and spheroid (right).  Circles: $t=0.05$ fixed, $\Delta p$ varied.
  Squares: $\Delta p=20$\,Pa fixed, $t$ varied.  Solid lines are the analytic
  predictions (sphere: $\sigma=R\,\Delta p/2t$; spheroid: global surface average
  of the axisymmetric formula).  Points from both routes overlap to four decimal
  places, confirming exact $\sigma\propto\Delta p/t$ scaling.}
  \label{fig:linearity}
\end{figure}

\noindent
The ratio $\sigma/(\Delta p/t)$ is constant to floating-point precision across
all six combinations (sphere: $0.5316$; spheroid $\sigma_1$: $0.7188$),
confirming that the solver is exactly linear in $\Delta p$ and exactly inversely
proportional to $t$ by construction.  The small offset from the sphere analytic
($0.5316$ vs $0.500$) is the systematic null-mode bias already quantified in
\S\ref{sec:benchmarks} — it is constant across all runs, not a linearity error.

\subsection{Equilibrium residual}
\label{sec:resid}

The relative residual
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon = \frac{\|L\vv{s}-\vv{b}\|}{\|\vv{b}\|}
\end{equation}
should be $\lesssim10^{-2}$ on smooth analytic meshes and
$\lesssim0.2$ on real irregular meshes (which have coarser, non-uniform
stencils).  Values significantly above these thresholds signal either
a stencil quality problem (too few neighbours, poor conditioning) or a
breakdown of the membrane assumption.

The test was run on all three analytic geometries at subdiv-4,
$\Delta p=20$\,Pa, $t=0.05$, depth\,$=3$, $\lambda=0.05$
(Table~\ref{tab:resid}).  In addition to the global $\varepsilon$ we
compute the \emph{per-vertex} residual
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon_i = \frac{\bigl\|(L\vv{s}-\vv{b})_i\bigr\|}{|\vv{b}_i|}
                = \frac{1}{\Delta p}
                  \Bigl(\textstyle\sum_{a}(L\vv{s}-\vv{b})_{a,i}^2\Bigr)^{1/2},
\end{equation}
which localises where equilibrium is least well satisfied
(Figure~\ref{fig:resid}).

\subsection{Shear diagnostic on a non-symmetric surface}

On the real HH17/HH20 meshes, compute the alignment diagnostic
$\delta=|r|/(|p|+|q|)$ at every vertex and map it onto the surface.  For
regions of strong geometric anisotropy (large $|\kappa_1-\kappa_2|$) a
large $\delta$ signals that the stress principal axes have rotated
substantially away from the curvature axes — a non-trivial, biologically
interesting result.  For nearly isotropic regions $\delta$ should remain
small.  A spatially random $\delta$ field would suggest numerical noise
rather than a physical signal.

\subsection{Smoothing sensitivity}

Repeat the solve with $\alpha\in\{0.3,0.5,0.7\}$ and
$n_{\rm iter}\in\{6,12,24\}$.  The key observables (locations of stress
maxima, anisotropy ratio $\sigma_1/\sigma_2$) should be stable across this
range; only the amplitude of the icosahedral artefacts should change.
If the location of the stress maximum shifts with smoothing, the underlying
signal may be too weak relative to the null-mode noise.

\subsection{Regularisation tradeoff and mesh-resolution requirement for embryo data}
\label{sec:resolution-requirement}

Two coupled questions decide whether a stress estimate on a real embryo
mesh is trustworthy: \emph{what regularisation weight $\lambda$ to use},
and \emph{how fine the mesh must be}.  Both were quantified on the analytic
geometries, where the exact stress is known (Figure~\ref{fig:resolution}).

\paragraph{The $\lambda$ tradeoff has a genuine optimum.}
The residual $\varepsilon$ alone is a misleading objective: at $\lambda=0$
on the sphere it collapses to $\varepsilon\approx10^{-7}$ while the stress
error explodes to ${\sim}1900\%$ — the unregularised solve is entirely
null modes (Figure~\ref{fig:resolution}A).  Sweeping $\lambda$ on the
spheroid (which, unlike the sphere, carries real hoop/meridional
anisotropy) reveals a U-shaped error curve: too small $\lambda$ leaves
null-mode contamination ($\sigma_{\max}$ error $6.6\%$ at $\lambda=0.005$),
too large $\lambda$ over-smooths the genuine anisotropy ($\sigma_{\max}$
error $5.8\%$ at $\lambda=0.2$).  The minimum sits at
$\lambda\approx0.05$--$0.1$ ($\sigma_{\max}$ error $0.9\%$,
$\sigma_{\min}$ error $3.4\%$), confirming the standing default
$\lambda=0.05$ as near-optimal.  The residual at that optimum is
$\varepsilon\approx2\times10^{-2}$ — i.e.\ the ``correct'' residual is
\emph{not} the smallest achievable one.

\paragraph{Resolution measured in $h\kappa$.}
The relevant resolution variable is the dimensionless product
$h\kappa$ (mesh spacing $\times$ local curvature), equivalently the number
of elements per radius of curvature $1/(h\kappa)$.
Figure~\ref{fig:resolution}B plots the smoothed stress error against
$h\kappa$ for the sphere (worst case: isotropic, so the error is pure
null-mode bias) and the spheroid (anisotropic, more embryo-like).  The
sphere needs $h\kappa\lesssim0.05$ (${\sim}20$ elements per curvature
radius) to reach ${\sim}3\%$ error; the spheroid is more forgiving,
already $<4\%$ at $h\kappa\approx0.18$.

\paragraph{Where the HH20 embryo mesh sits.}
The decimated HH20 mesh ($n=3766$) has median $h\kappa\approx0.17$ and
$p_{90}\approx0.44$ — \emph{coarser} than our coarsest analytic test
(sphere subdiv-3, $h\kappa=0.14$).  In high-curvature, locally
isotropic regions (closing neural folds) this implies a worst-case error
$\gtrsim10\%$; in the smoother tube-like body it is closer to the
spheroid's few-percent.  To bring the worst regions to $h\kappa\approx0.05$
by \emph{uniform} refinement would require $n\!\sim\!(0.17/0.05)^2\!\times\!3766
\approx 4\times10^{4}$ vertices (and ${\sim}3\times10^{5}$ to fix the
$p_{90}$ tail) — impractical uniformly.  The practical recommendations are:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Use \textbf{curvature-adaptive refinement}: add resolution only
        where $h\kappa$ is large (the folds), not uniformly.
  \item Keep $\lambda=0.05$ and the 12-iteration Laplacian post-smoothing.
  \item Export a per-vertex $h\kappa$ \emph{resolution-quality map} with the
        stress, and flag vertices with $h\kappa\gtrsim0.1$ as
        lower-confidence — they are below ${\sim}10$ elements per curvature
        radius, where the GFDM quadratic fit degrades.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Computational cost and scaling.}
The refinement target is constrained not only by accuracy but by solve
time (Table~\ref{tab:timing}).  The solve forms the normal equations
$(L^\top L+\lambda^2 R^\top R)$ and factors them with a sparse direct
solver up to a DOF threshold, switching to iterative \texttt{lsqr} above
it.  The direct path scales steeply — roughly $\times35$ in time per
$\times4$ in DOFs — because the depth-3 GFDM stencils ($\sim$30 non-zeros
per row) make $L^\top L$ relatively dense ($\sim$900 non-zeros per row),
so the factorisation suffers heavy fill-in.  In practice this means a
subdiv-5 mesh ($\sim$10\,k vertices) already costs several minutes on the
direct path, while the iterative solver handles a four-times-larger
subdiv-6 mesh in comparable time.  The current crossover threshold
($60\,000$ DOFs) is set too high — subdiv-5 stays on the slow direct path
when \texttt{lsqr} would be faster; lowering it to ${\sim}20$--$30\,000$
DOFs is a cheap improvement.  The HH20 embryo ($n=3766$, ${\sim}11\,$k
DOFs) solves in ${\sim}15$--$30$\,s; a $4\times10^{4}$-vertex
adaptively-refined target would sit in the subdiv-6 regime
(${\sim}10$\,min with \texttt{lsqr}).

\section{Biological interpretation: the neural-tube tension landscape}
\label{sec:neuraltube}

The validation sections establish that the GFDM solve recovers the correct
membrane stress on geometries where the answer is known.  This section turns to
the harder question of \emph{what the inferred tension landscape means} on a real
embryonic organ — the chick neural tube — and what can and cannot be concluded
from equilibrium alone.  Two recent works bracket our method from opposite sides
and, read together, suggest a small number of genuinely novel, testable
statements about neural-tube mechanics.  We use them as anchors: Romo
\textit{et al.} (2014), a full-field experimental stress analysis of inflated
aneurysm tissue, and Bal \textit{et al.} (2026), a first-principles active-shell
continuum theory coarse-grained from a 3D vertex model.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Two reference points sharing one equilibrium core}
\label{sec:nt-anchors}

\paragraph{Romo \textit{et al.} (2014) --- passive inference on a pressurised
membrane.}  Layers of human ascending thoracic aortic aneurysm are inflated in a
bulge test; the deformed shape is captured by stereo digital image correlation;
and the local stress field is recovered by solving the membrane equilibrium
equation on the observed surface,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{\sqrt g}\bigl(\sqrt g\,\sigma^{\alpha\beta}\vv g_\alpha\bigr)_{,\beta}
  + p\,\nn = \vv 0,
  \label{eq:romo}
\end{equation}
discretised with triangular shape functions and solved by least squares.
Equation~\eqref{eq:romo} is \emph{identical} to our balance
law~\eqref{eq:equil}; the authors state plainly that ``the tension in the wall
can be determined without the use of a constitutive model'' --- precisely the
static determinacy of \S\ref{sec:el-determinacy}, here validated on real tissue
(interior errors $<1.5\%$).  Their method nonetheless requires an \emph{open}
domain with prescribed boundary tractions, exactly as in cMSM
(Mar\'in-Llaurad\'o \textit{et al.}, 2023).

\paragraph{Bal \textit{et al.} (2026) --- active-shell forward theory.}  A
continuum shell theory is derived by coarse-graining a 3D vertex model whose
faces are active viscoelastic gel surfaces.  The resulting surface Cauchy stress
(their Eq.~2.19) splits additively into a passive elastic part and an active
contractile part,
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol\sigma = \boldsymbol\sigma^{e} + \xi\rho\,\vv g,
  \label{eq:bal-stress}
\end{equation}
with $\xi$ the myosin activity and $\rho$ the cortical density, and the balance
of momentum (their Eq.~2.20) reads $\nabla\!\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma=0$ tangentially
and $\boldsymbol\sigma\!:\!\vv k=0$ normally --- again our
\eqref{eq:tang-equil} and \eqref{eq:laplace-tensor}, with the explicit remark
that the right-hand side becomes non-zero under a transmural pressure difference.
Crucially, they show that an \emph{apicobasal asymmetry} of the active tension
$\xi$ generates bending, and that a Cosserat (through-thickness) kinematics ---
not a pure membrane --- is required to capture it.

\paragraph{The lineage we complete.}  Romo \textit{et al.} (open dome, passive),
cMSM (open epithelial domes, active but boundary-pinned), and the present method
are the \emph{same} inverse procedure: solve the statically determinate membrane
balance on an observed pressurised shape.  What none of them addresses is the
\emph{closed, arbitrary-topology} case --- which is exactly the geometry of the
neural tube.  Filling that gap, with the closed-surface null-mode treatment of
\S\ref{sec:null-modes}, is the methodological contribution that makes a
neural-tube tension landscape accessible at all.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{What equilibrium fixes, and what it cannot split}
\label{sec:nt-decomposition}

Static determinacy (\S\ref{sec:el-determinacy}) guarantees that the inferred
resultant $\Nb$ is unique --- it is the \emph{total} membrane tension that
balances the lumen pressure.  But equation~\eqref{eq:bal-stress} decomposes that
total into a passive elastic and an active contractile contribution,
\begin{equation}
  \Nb = \Nb_{\rm elastic} + \Nb_{\rm active},
  \qquad
  \Nb_{\rm active} = t\,\xi\rho\,\vv g .
  \label{eq:nt-split}
\end{equation}
Equilibrium constrains the sum but is blind to the split: the same $\Nb$ can be
produced by a stiff, weakly contractile wall or a compliant, strongly
contractile one.  This is the central honest statement about a \emph{living}
tube.  Isolating $\Nb_{\rm active}$ requires either an independent measurement
(laser ablation, phospho-myosin intensity) or a Bal-type forward model with
measured material parameters.  The inference therefore delivers a hard
\emph{upper-envelope} constraint on tissue tension, not a decomposition --- a
limitation that is itself a result, and one that sharply distinguishes the
inert-tissue setting of Romo \textit{et al.} from the embryo.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Two equilibrium-derived signatures of active tension}
\label{sec:nt-signatures}

Although equilibrium cannot \emph{quantify} the split~\eqref{eq:nt-split}, two
fields the pipeline already computes act as qualitative \emph{detectors} of the
active term --- because each takes a value that a purely passive, pressurised
membrane is forbidden to produce.

\paragraph{(i) Tilt of the principal stress axes off the curvature axes.}
On an axisymmetric surface under pressure alone, symmetry locks the principal
stress directions to the curvature directions, so the shear DOF vanishes,
$r=0$, and the alignment diagnostic
$\delta=|r|/(|p|+|q|)$ of equation~\eqref{eq:alignment} is near zero (confirmed:
$1$--$2\%$ on sphere and spheroid, \S\ref{sec:stress-dirs}).  A \emph{systematic}
tilt of $\vv d_1$ away from the curvature frame --- a coherent, non-random
$\delta>0$ field --- cannot arise from an isotropic pressure load on a
revolution; it signals a directional tension whose axis is set by something other
than the local geometry, i.e.\ oriented active stress (for example
convergent-extension forces along the anteroposterior axis).  The shear $r$,
which we previously reported only as a small diagnostic, is thus the natural
read-out of \emph{anisotropic active tension}: passive aneurysm tissue (Romo
\textit{et al.}) would show $\vv d_1$ locked to the curvature axes, whereas an
actively remodelling tube need not.

\paragraph{(ii) Compressive principal stress.}
A convex surface under positive internal pressure is in tension along
\emph{both} principal directions: the Young--Laplace
relation~\eqref{eq:laplace} with $\kappa_1,\kappa_2>0$ and $\Delta p>0$ cannot
produce $\sigma<0$.  Inferred \emph{compression} ($\sigma_{\min}<0$), which we do
observe on HH20, therefore cannot be a pressure response on a convex patch; via
equation~\eqref{eq:bal-stress} it is a signature of the active term overwhelming
the pressure term, or of incipient buckling --- the same active/bending regime
Bal \textit{et al.} require a Cosserat shell to describe.  Compressive zones are
thus candidate markers of the contractile, fold-forming machinery rather than of
load bearing.

Both signatures invert the usual reading of a stress map: the
\emph{morphogenetically} interesting vertices are not those of largest tension but
those where the axes tilt or the minor stress turns compressive.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Where membrane inference ends and bending begins}
\label{sec:nt-bending}

Bal \textit{et al.} locate the breakdown of membrane theory precisely where
apicobasal active asymmetry drives bending --- the hinge points and closing
folds.  These are high-curvature regions, and they coincide exactly with where
our own numerics degrade: the resolution analysis (\S\ref{sec:resolution-requirement})
flags vertices with $h\kappa\gtrsim0.1$ as low-confidence, and the HH20 folds sit
at $h\kappa\approx0.44$ ($p_{90}$).  The numerical limit and the physical limit
therefore fall on the \emph{same} regions: where the membrane idealisation is
least valid (bending dominates, after Bal) is where the GFDM quadratic fit is
also least reliable.  This lets us state the domain of validity cleanly rather
than as an apology --- membrane inference is quantitative on the smooth tube body
and becomes a qualitative fold-detector at the hinges, which is exactly where an
active Cosserat-shell forward model (Bal-type) would take over.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{From a stress map to a vulnerability map}
\label{sec:nt-vulnerability}

The headline biological finding of Romo \textit{et al.} is that aneurysm
\emph{rupture does not occur at the location of maximum stress}: it initiates in a
\emph{weakened} zone identified by strain localisation and local wall
\emph{thinning}, and accounting for that thinning (via incompressibility) roughly
doubled the inferred stress relative to a constant-thickness estimate.  The
mechanically decisive quantity is stress \emph{relative to local capacity}, not
absolute stress.

Translated to morphogenesis, the decisive sites for neural-tube shape change
need not be the peak-tension sites either; they are where tension coincides with a
locally compliant, thinned, or actively remodelling zone.  This argues for
reporting not a bare $\sigma$ field but a composite \emph{vulnerability /
event-likelihood} landscape, overlaying
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item the inferred principal tensions $\sigma_1,\sigma_2$ (and the resultants
        $N_1,N_2$, which are thickness-independent, \S\ref{sec:thickness});
  \item a measured thickness field $t(\vv x)$ --- the same heterogeneity Romo
        \textit{et al.} found decisive, and the reason a real $t(\vv x)$ (not the
        placeholder divisor) is the priority next input;
  \item the two active signatures of \S\ref{sec:nt-signatures} (axis tilt
        $\delta$, compressive $\sigma_{\min}$);
  \item the $h\kappa$ confidence map of \S\ref{sec:resolution-requirement}.
\end{itemize}
This is the direct morphogenetic analogue of the Romo ``stress~$+$~thinning''
rupture map.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{A testable thesis for the HH17\,$\to$\,HH20 transition}
\label{sec:nt-thesis}

The pieces assemble into a single, falsifiable claim.  The chick neural tube is a
closed, pressurised, active epithelial shell; equilibrium inference recovers its
\emph{total} membrane-tension landscape without a constitutive model (extending
Romo-style and cMSM inference from open domes to closed topology).  Because living
tension is the sum of a pressure-equilibrating and an actively generated part
(Bal \textit{et al.}), and equilibrium fixes only the sum, the active component
must be read indirectly --- through the two forbidden-for-passive signatures of
\S\ref{sec:nt-signatures}.  The prediction is then:
\begin{quote}\itshape
the increase in tension magnitude and anisotropy we observe from HH17 to HH20
(\,$\sigma_{\max}$ rising ${\sim}1.9\times$, $\sigma_{\min}$ becoming
${\sim}2.8\times$ more compressive\,) is accompanied by a growing principal-axis
tilt $\delta$ and by compressive $\sigma_{\min}$ zones that localise to the
high-curvature folds; these active-signature regions, not the peak-tension
regions, mark the sites of active shape change.
\end{quote}
Each clause is checkable against the saved per-vertex fields, and the contrast
with passive aneurysm tissue (where $\delta\!\to\!0$ and $\sigma_{\min}>0$
everywhere) makes the active interpretation discriminating rather than
descriptive.

\section{Stress-based finite-element discretisation}
\label{sec:fem}

This section presents the \emph{primary} numerical engine of this work: a
stress-based finite-element discretisation of the membrane balance
law~\eqref{eq:equil}, implemented in \texttt{membrane\_stress\_fem.py}.  It is
the method of choice for closed and arbitrary-topology surfaces because it is
faster, more accurate, and better scaled to embryo-resolution meshes than the
generalised finite-difference (GFDM) scheme of Section~\ref{sec:gfdm}.

Three structural advantages drive the choice.  (i)~The P1 stiffness $K$ couples
only 1-ring neighbours (${\sim}57$ non-zeros per row in $K^\top K$ vs
${\sim}376$ for the depth-3 GFDM normal equations), making it $5$--$12\times$
faster with no per-vertex weighted-least-squares operator to build.
(ii)~Accuracy is at least as good as GFDM on every metric at the same mesh and
$\lambda$, and better on most.
(iii)~Putting the cMSM formulation (Mar\'in-Llaurad\'o et al., 2023) --- itself
a stress-based P1 FEM, restricted to open domes with a global parametrisation
--- on a \emph{closed} surface confirms directly that the ``lines'' artefact and
singularity of $K$ are intrinsic to the static indeterminacy of closed surfaces,
not to the GFDM stencils.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Primal (virtual-work) weak form}
\label{sec:fem-weak}

Dotting the equilibrium~\eqref{eq:equil} with a vector test field $\vv w$,
integrating over the closed surface, and integrating by parts via the surface
divergence theorem (no boundary term on a closed $\Gamma$) gives the
\emph{principle of virtual work} with the stress as the unknown:
\begin{equation}
  a(\NN,\vv w)\;=\;\int_\Gamma \NN : \bm\varepsilon_s(\vv w)\,dS
  \;=\;\int_\Gamma \Delta p\,(\nn\cdot\vv w)\,dS\;=\;\ell(\vv w),
  \qquad
  \bm\varepsilon_s(\vv w)=\tfrac12\bigl(\nabla_{\!s}\vv w+\nabla_{\!s}\vv w^{\!\top}\bigr),
  \label{eq:fem-weak}
\end{equation}
for all admissible $\vv w$: internal virtual work (stress $:$ virtual strain)
equals the external virtual work of the pressure.  This is the same balance as
the GFDM residual, written variationally rather than by collocation.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Finite-element spaces and assembly}
\label{sec:fem-assembly}

Both fields use continuous piecewise-linear (P1) interpolation on the surface
triangulation, in ambient Cartesian components.

\paragraph{Trial space (stress).}  Per vertex $i$ the three DOFs $(p_i,q_i,r_i)$
encode $\NN_i = p_i\,\vv v_1\tp\vv v_1 + q_i\,\vv v_2\tp\vv v_2
+ r_i(\vv v_1\tp\vv v_2+\vv v_2\tp\vv v_1)$ in the local frame
$(\vv v_1,\vv v_2,\nn)$ (Section~\ref{sec:geometry}), so $\NN_i\nn_i=\vv0$ by
construction.  The field is $\NN(\vv x)=\sum_i\varphi_i(\vv x)\,\NN_i$.

\paragraph{Test space (virtual displacement).}  Continuous P1 vector fields with
ambient nodal basis $\{\vv e_d\,\varphi_j\}$, $d\in\{1,2,3\}$.  With $n$ vertices
this gives $3n$ trial DOFs and $3n$ test functions --- a \emph{square}
$3n\times3n$ system, exactly the count of cMSM.

\paragraph{Element block.}  On a triangle $T$ of area $A_T$, the hat-function
surface gradient $\vv g_j=\nabla_{\!s}\varphi_j|_T = (\nn_T\times(\vv x_k-\vv
x_i))/(2A_T)$ is constant and lies in the plane of $T$.  For the test function
$\vv w=\vv e_d\varphi_j$, $\bm\varepsilon_s(\vv w)=\mathrm{sym}(\vv e_d\tp\vv
g_j)$ and, since $\NN$ is symmetric, $\NN:\bm\varepsilon_s(\vv w)=(\NN\,\vv
g_j)_d$.  Using $\int_T\varphi_m\,dS=A_T/3$, the element contribution is
\begin{equation}
  a\big|_T(\NN,\,\vv e_d\varphi_j)
  = \frac{A_T}{3}\sum_{m\in T}\bigl(\NN_m\,\vv g_j\bigr)_d,
  \label{eq:fem-block}
\end{equation}
which expands through each node's frame into the $(p_m,q_m,r_m)$ columns and
scatters into the global stiffness $K$.

\paragraph{Load vector.}  With nodal outward normals $\nn_m$ and the P1 mass
matrix $M^T_{jm}=\tfrac{A_T}{12}(1+\delta_{jm})$,
\begin{equation}
  \ell(\vv e_d\varphi_j)=\Delta p\sum_T\sum_{m\in T} n_{m,d}\,M^T_{jm}.
  \label{eq:fem-load}
\end{equation}

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Solving the singular system and recovering stresses}
\label{sec:fem-solve}

$K$ is square but \emph{singular} on a closed surface: its null space is the
finite-dimensional space of self-equilibrating stress states
(Section~\ref{sec:null-modes}).  The load~\eqref{eq:fem-load} is nonetheless
consistent, because the net pressure force and torque on a closed surface vanish
(the right null space of $K^\top$ are the rigid virtual displacements, for which
$\ell=0$).  The solve proceeds in two stages:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),nosep]
  \item \textbf{Raw, minimum-norm.}  Solve $K\vv s=\vv b$ with \texttt{lsqr},
        which returns the minimum-norm least-squares solution (no regularisation).
        The headline diagnostic is whether the raw field carries the same
        triangulation ``lines'' as the raw GFDM solve.
  \item \textbf{Regularised.}  Minimise $\|K\vv s-\vv b\|^2+w^2\|R\vv s\|^2$ with a
        \emph{FEM-native} roughness $R$ --- the area-weighted P1 surface gradient
        of the ambient stress components, $\|R\vv s\|^2=\int_\Gamma\sum_{ab}
        |\nabla_s N_{ab}|^2\,dS$, built from the same element gradients $\vv g_j$
        and the per-vertex frames (no GFDM operators).  Because the virtual-work
        $K$ is area-weighted (entries ${\sim}h$) while $R\sim O(1)$, a bare
        $\lambda$ would mis-scale by ${\sim}h^{-4}$ and collapse the solution; we
        set $w=\lambda\,\|K\|_F/\|R\|_F$ so that $\lambda=0.05$ is the same
        dimensionless weight as in the GFDM solve.  Small systems factor the normal
        equations $(K^\top K+w^2R^\top R)$ directly; large ones use iterative
        \texttt{lsqr} on $[\,K;\,wR\,]$ (the sparse P1 $K$ makes this scale gently).
\end{enumerate}
Principal stresses are then $\sigma_1,\sigma_2 = \mathrm{eig}
\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}p&r\\ r&q\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]/t$, identical
post-processing to the GFDM pipeline.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Design decisions and validation plan}
\label{sec:fem-decisions}

The configuration is fixed as follows (alternatives weighed in the working
notes):
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Formulation:} primal virtual-work~\eqref{eq:fem-weak} ---
        the faithful cMSM-style stress FEM.  A \emph{least-squares} FEM (SPD,
        constitutive-free, the closest twin of GFDM) and a \emph{mixed
        Hellinger--Reissner} scheme (structurally null-mode-free, but requiring a
        compliance tensor --- hence not constitutive-free --- and
        symmetric-stress $H(\mathrm{div})$ surface elements with no package
        support) were the runners-up.
  \item \textbf{Representation:} P1 nodal stress in the per-vertex local frame
        ($3$ DOF, identical to GFDM, for a like-for-like comparison).
  \item \textbf{Regulariser:} a FEM-native 1-ring roughness
        ($\int|\nabla_s N|^2$ from the P1 element gradients), Frobenius-matched
        to $K$ so that $\lambda$ is a dimensionless relative weight independent
        of mesh spacing.
  \item \textbf{Implementation:} hand-rolled with \texttt{scipy.sparse}
        (consistent with the no-framework rule; the symmetric-tangential-tensor
        unknown is non-standard, so the tensor integrands are custom regardless
        of package), reusing the existing mesh, frame, analytic-reference and
        plotting infrastructure.
  \item \textbf{Mesh:} IcoSphere, consistent with the rest of the suite.
\end{itemize}

\label{sec:fem-reg}

--- the closed-surface analogue of their open spherical cap, chosen because it carries genuine
anisotropy so that \emph{over}-regularization penalizes the solution and a true optimum exists
(on a closed \emph{sphere} the exact field is constant, so more smoothing always helps and no
optimum appears).  Figure~\ref{fig:femreg} collects the analysis.

  \label{fig:femreg}

Two conclusions carry over to our setting.  First, the optimal weight ($\lambda\approx0.3$
for the FEM on the closed prolate spheroid) must be chosen per method and geometry: the
FEM-native 1-ring roughness and the closed-surface geometry require stronger smoothing
before genuine anisotropy is over-flattened, and the trace error in particular is
insensitive to $\lambda$ over a wide range (the plateau in panel~a).  The L-curve
corner is an appropriate operational selector when no ground truth is available.
Second, panel~(d) shows a clear knee that coincides with the onset of the residual
rise in panel~(c); this is the regularization-selection rule for experimental data,
where the analytic error of panel~(a) is unavailable.

\paragraph{Automatic selection per mesh.}  Because $\lambda$ is dimensionless
(Frobenius-matched, Section~\ref{sec:fem-solve}) it is invariant under refinement of a
fixed geometry, but it does depend on the curvature distribution, so a genuinely new
shape warrants its own choice.  This is automated by \texttt{solve\_membrane\_fem(...,
lam="auto")}: the solver runs a one-off $\lambda$ sweep, measures the residual
$\|K\vv s-\vv b\|/\|\vv b\|$ at each, and returns the value at the \emph{onset of the
residual rise} --- the elbow (maximum distance from the endpoint chord) of the
$(\log\lambda,\log\mathrm{residual})$ curve, which is cMSM's panel-(c) rule and needs
no ground truth.  On the closed surfaces here this selector is deliberately
\emph{conservative}: the residual lifts off before the full-$\sigma$ error reaches its
minimum, so the automatic $\lambda$ ($\sim\!10^{-2}$) sits below the error-optimal
$\sim\!10^{-1}$, favouring equilibrium fidelity (a low residual and a sharp
$\sigma_{\max}$) at the cost of a noisier $\sigma_{\min}$.  The chosen value and the
full sweep are returned (and reported) so it can be inspected or overridden.

%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{How our regularization differs from cMSM's}
\label{sec:fem-reg-vs-cmsm}

The reference method (Mar\'in-Llaurad\'o et al., 2023, Supplementary Note~2.3.1) and
ours both regularize the same statically-indeterminate balance, but the choice of
penalty differs in what it smooths, how many parameters it carries, and which
physical constraints it enforces.  Because cMSM is the method we adapt, it is worth
making the difference explicit.

\paragraph{The cMSM penalty.}  cMSM minimise
$E(\vv u)=\tfrac12\|A\vv u-\vv b\|^2+L(\vv u)$ where $\vv u$ collects the nodal
stress components.  They first dismiss the zeroth-order (magnitude) Tikhonov term
\begin{equation}
  L_0(\sigma)=\frac{\lambda_0^2}{2}\int_\Gamma \sigma:\sigma\,dS,
  \label{eq:cmsm-L0}
\end{equation}
because penalising the \emph{magnitude} of the tension produces solutions with sharp
gradients.  They instead use \emph{two first-order} terms.  The first penalises
gradients of the mean (hydrostatic) tension,
\begin{equation}
  L_{1t}(\sigma)=\frac{\lambda_t^2}{2}\int_\Gamma
  \nabla\,\mathrm{trace}(\sigma)\cdot\nabla\,\mathrm{trace}(\sigma)\,dS,
  \label{eq:cmsm-L1t}
\end{equation}
and the second penalises the \emph{curl} (swirl) of the tension field, which
$L_{1t}$ alone leaves unconstrained,
\begin{equation}
  L_{1c}(\sigma)=\frac{\lambda_c^2}{2}\int_\Gamma
  \mathrm{curl}\,\sigma:\mathrm{curl}\,\sigma\,dS,
  \label{eq:cmsm-L1c}
\end{equation}
giving the combined quadratic penalty
$L(\vv u)=\tfrac12\vv u^\top(\lambda_t^2 Q_t+\lambda_c^2 Q_c)\vv u$ with two
independent weights $\lambda_t,\lambda_c$.  For experimental data they further impose
the \emph{inequality constraint}
\begin{equation}
  \det\sigma_J=\sigma^{11}_J\sigma^{22}_J-(\sigma^{12}_J)^2>0
  \quad\text{at every node }J,
  \label{eq:cmsm-detpos}
\end{equation}
which forbids the negative principal tensions that flat or concave ($K\le0$) patches
cannot physically sustain; this turns the minimisation nonlinear and is solved with a
constrained optimiser (\texttt{fmincon}).

\paragraph{Our penalty.}  We use a \emph{single} first-order term: the squared surface
gradient of \emph{all} ambient stress components,
\begin{equation}
  \|R\vv s\|^2=\int_\Gamma\sum_{ab}\bigl|\nabla_s N_{ab}\bigr|^2\,dS,
  \label{eq:our-rough}
\end{equation}
weighted by the single dimensionless $\lambda$ through $w=\lambda\,\|K\|_F/\|R\|_F$
(Section~\ref{sec:fem-solve}), and we solve the resulting \emph{linear} normal
equations $(K^\top K+w^2R^\top R)\vv s=K^\top\vv b$ with no inequality constraint.

\paragraph{The conceptual difference.}  The full-gradient norm~\eqref{eq:our-rough}
couples every first-order differential invariant of the symmetric tensor field --- its
\emph{divergence}, its \emph{trace gradient}, and its \emph{curl} --- in one quadratic
form, whereas cMSM \emph{isolate} the trace gradient~\eqref{eq:cmsm-L1t} and the
curl~\eqref{eq:cmsm-L1c} and weight them independently while \emph{excluding} the
divergence.  Their exclusion of the divergence is deliberate: the surface divergence
of $\NN$ \emph{is} the physics (it equals the pressure load through
equilibrium~\eqref{eq:equil}), so it belongs in the data term $\|A\vv u-\vv b\|^2$, not
in the smoother.  Our single norm therefore also lightly penalises the divergence;
because $K$ already drives the divergence toward the pressure, the overlap is mild, but
it is the reason our optimal $\lambda$ sits higher and our trace error is the
flattest-responding quantity (the trace gradient is the most heavily-penalised
sub-mode).  The two-parameter cMSM form is the more surgical instrument --- in
particular their $\lambda_c$ removes short-wavelength swirl that a pure trace penalty
misses --- at the cost of a second weight to calibrate.

\paragraph{Why it matters less than the boundary.}  We ported cMSM's exact
$\lambda_t+\lambda_c$ penalty into our closed-surface solve (the covariant derivative
needs no Christoffel symbols in ambient components,
$\nabla_\alpha N_{\beta\gamma}=(v_\beta)_a(v_\gamma)_b\,(G_\alpha N)_{ab}$).  On the
closed sphere and spheroid it performs no better than a plain first-order Tikhonov: a
$\lambda_t/\lambda_c$ sweep plateaus the spurious anisotropy at ${\sim}10\%$ and the
sphere deviatoric is essentially unimproved.  The reason is structural.  cMSM regularize
an \emph{open} dome with a pinned basal boundary and a global conformal
parametrisation: the boundary pins the divergence-free null space
(Section~\ref{sec:null-modes}), so the regularizer only has to tidy residual gradients
and swirl.  A closed surface has no such boundary; its null mode tracks the
low-wavenumber mesh (icosahedral) pattern, which \emph{no} gradient penalty can remove
without flattening the genuine signal at the same wavelength.  Hence on closed surfaces
the \emph{form} of the first-order penalty (one combined norm vs.\ split
trace$+$curl) is secondary --- what is load-bearing is the open-boundary pinning that
closed surfaces lack, which is exactly why the regularised solve must be paired with a
mild Laplacian post-smoothing or run at the higher $\lambda$ of
Figure~\ref{fig:femreg}.

\paragraph{Compressive tensions: constraint vs.\ signal.}  A final difference of intent
concerns~\eqref{eq:cmsm-detpos}.  cMSM treat $\det\sigma\le0$ (a negative principal
tension) as an artefact to be projected away, appropriate for the passive dome shapes
they analyse.  We deliberately do \emph{not} impose it: on the neural tube a genuinely
compressive $\sigma_{\min}<0$ is one of the two equilibrium-derived signatures of
\emph{active} tension we wish to detect (Section~\ref{sec:nt-signatures}).
Enforcing positive-definiteness would suppress exactly the quantity of biological
interest, so we keep the solve linear and read $\sigma_{\min}<0$ as data, flagging
low-confidence high-$h\kappa$ regions separately (Section~\ref{sec:resolution-requirement})
rather than constraining them.

\subsection{Is Laplacian post-smoothing worth a second knob? A $\lambda\times$iters study}
\label{sec:fem-smoothing}

The GFDM pipeline removed the residual low-frequency streaks with a Laplacian
(umbrella) post-smoothing pass,
$\sigma\leftarrow(1-\alpha)\sigma+\alpha\,\bar\sigma_{1\text{-ring}}$
(Section~\ref{sec:tikhonov}).  Applied to the FEM solution this is a \emph{second}
regularization knob that overlaps with the Tikhonov weight $\lambda$ --- both are
low-pass filters of the same null modes.  Its two parameters (blend $\alpha$,
iteration count) collapse to a single effective smoothing scale: one pass is the
linear operator $M=(1-\alpha)I+\alpha A$ with $A$ the row-normalised 1-ring average,
and after $m$ passes a mode of adjacency eigenvalue $\mu$ decays like
$[(1-\alpha)+\alpha\mu]^m\approx e^{-m\alpha(1-\mu)}$, so only the product $m\alpha$
matters.  We therefore fix $\alpha=0.5$ and sweep the iteration count $m$.

To decide whether this second knob earns its place we ran, for each shape, a joint
sweep of $\lambda$ (the same $10^{-4}$ to $50$ as above) and
$m\in\{0,4,8,12,16\}$, smoothing the \emph{DOF} fields $(p,q,r)$ and recomputing the
residual and roughness on the smoothed solution so that both respond to $m$ as they
do to $\lambda$ (\texttt{fem\_smoothing\_sweep.py}).  Errors are measured against the
analytic field over the same equatorial belt / cylinder body as before.
Figures~\ref{fig:femsmooth-sphere}--\ref{fig:femsmooth-capsule} show the four shapes;
Table~\ref{tab:femsmooth} reports the optimum.  In every panel set the layout mirrors
cMSM Supplementary Fig.~15 --- (a) relative error vs $\lambda$, (b)
$\sigma_1=\sigma_{\max}$ vs latitude/axial coordinate at the best $\lambda$ with the
analytic solution overlaid, (c) force-balance residual vs $\lambda$, (d) the L-curve
--- and colour encodes the smoothing iteration count (dark ${=}\,0$, light ${=}\,16$).

\paragraph{Conclusion: one knob, not two.}  On every surface that carries a
\emph{genuine} stress field --- the prolate and oblate spheroids and the capsule ---
the optimum is at zero smoothing iterations: Laplacian post-smoothing cannot lower the
error below what the Tikhonov weight already achieves, because any pass that
suppresses the spurious anisotropy also flattens the real meridional/hoop variation at
the same wavelength, and it always \emph{raises} the equilibrium residual (panel~c).
The sphere is the lone exception, and a misleading one: its exact field is constant
isotropic, so there is no true signal for smoothing to damage and more iterations
monotonically help --- the same degeneracy that makes the sphere a poor $\lambda$
calibration target (Section~\ref{sec:fem-reg}).  We therefore regularize the FEM with
a \emph{single} principled knob --- the Tikhonov weight $\lambda$, selected at the
L-curve corner --- and \emph{drop} the Laplacian post-smoothing inherited from the
GFDM pipeline.  This both simplifies the method and removes a parameter that, away
from the sphere, only trades equilibrium for cosmetic smoothness.
